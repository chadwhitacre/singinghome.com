Announcing Rod's Final Book: Learning Greek Passage by Passage

Greetings, friends. 👋

I shared back in March that Rod was leaving us one final book project before he went singing home on May 22. That project is now complete, and the result is available for free download on WhitacreGreek.com and for purchase at cost on Amazon.

Learning Greek Passage by Passage is a hybrid between the common methods currently available and an inductive method. It begins with several lessons that provide students with an overview of the language, including major features of morphology and sentence structure. After the initial lessons there is a constant focus on the Greek text.

Rodney Alan Whitacre, December 28, 1949 – May 22, 2023

Rodney Alan Whitacre, 73, of Sewickley, Pennsylvania, went to be with the Lord on May 22, 2023. He passed peacefully in his home from lymphoma, with his loving family by his side.

Rod was born on December 28, 1949 to Charles and Leah (Keller) Whitacre in Des Moines, Iowa. He grew up in Richland, Washington, on the banks of the Columbia River in southeastern Washington State. When a child, Rod was baptized in the Methodist Church and fell in love with the Bible. By the time he was in junior high school he was active in a small inter-denominational mission and headed towards full-time ministry in the future.

After graduating from Richland High School, Washington in 1968, Rod went on to earn a Bachelor’s degree in Biblical and Theological Studies from Gordon College in Wenham, Massachusetts (1972), a Master of Theological Studies from Gordon-Conwell Theological Seminary in Hamilton, Massachusetts (1976), and a Ph.D. in New Testament from Cambridge University, England (1980). He was later awarded an honorary Doctor of Divinity from Cranmer Theological House, Dallas, Texas.

Already & Not Yet

If you've sat under Rod Whitacre's teaching for more than five minutes, you've likely heard the concept of the already and the not yet. We live between times, between Christ's first coming in humility and His second coming in glory. His Kingdom is already present with us, but not yet fully realized. If you've played roots music with Rod, it's likely been out of the Already & Not Yet songbook he lovingly compiled and edited over the past fifteen years.

About a week ago, around the time of John Rodgers' memorial service, my dad started what seems to be his final turning from the already of his life with us to the not yet of his heavenly home. Apples in a seed

Thanks for following along with Rod Whitacre's journey home, and welcome to those who signed up in the past few weeks. You can catch up on past emails in the archive. We're up over 200 subscribers. Rod has received messages from over 150 of you. I usually share your messages with him within a day or two of receiving them, even if I'm not able to reply right away.

My dad said recently, "Certainly a lot of these are surprising to me. I'm very thankful. It's too easy just to see your own failures and sin and not acknowledge the way God has used you to help others." Then he pulled out an aphorism I hadn't heard before: "You can count the seeds in an apple, but you can't count the apples in a seed." I thought that was fitting.

Thank you for your continued love and prayers! 🙏

The cancer is progressing. After a couple weeks of relative comfort, he recently increased his pain medication, because he has noticed pain spreading from his abdomen into his legs. Even so, he remains clear-minded (when he's awake), and his spirits are strong. “I’m surprisingly at peace with spending the rest of my life in this bed. That’s God’s mercy and the prayers of his people somehow.” Here's a picture of the view from his bed.

Thank you for your love and prayers

Rod greatly appreciates your messages of love and encouragement, and the memories you are sharing. I've read all of them to him through yesterday morning (we're up to about 80) though I'm still a little backlogged on sending out individual replies. Hopefully this weekend I can catch up. Thanks for your patience.

My dad is comfortable at home and sleeps most of the day. He's recovered enough clarity of mind to spend a few minutes a day putting the finishing touches on one final project, a workbook to accompany his Greek grammar. His inspiration is Bishop Schereschewsky, who translated the Bible into Chinese while "sitting for nearly twenty-five years in the same chair, slowly and painfully typing with two fingers."

We don't expect 25 years (though God's will be done!). According to a nurse, one likely scenario is that Rod will linger in his present state for several weeks, and then decline rapidly. My mother and I visited Sewickley Cemetery today. We found a "grave on a green hillside," just as my dad has requested. Jess and I live across the valley.

Learning to be loved

I've been gradually reading all of your wonderful email messages to my dad these past few days. At one point he was overwhelmed with emotion, "people really want to say something to me." One of the main lessons he learned from his arduous time in the hospital was what it means to be loved, by God and by others. "I've been talking about this stuff my whole life, now I'm really experiencing it." Thank you all for your great love for my dad!

Here are a couple pictures from yesterday of Rod with his kids and (most of) his grandkids. We're having some sweet moments together despite discomfort, lack of mobility and energy, and some difficulty breathing. Now that we're through the first flurry of activity moving back home and getting settled, we'll see how the days progress. I expect I'll check in again here late in the week or over the weekend.

Glory to God for all things!

Comfortable at home

Rod arrived back at the house on Thursday evening, March 2. He is comfortable in a hospital bed downstairs in the family room, with a little bit of oxygen and regular medication, primarily for pain management. He has a nice view into the back yard with trees and birds, and he has his iPod with lots of favorite songs that he enjoys listening to. His grandkids have been able to visit and he is having final conversations with them and others in the family. Rod asks your forgiveness for any hurt he has caused.

We are not sure how long he has left with us. It could be days, or weeks, or possibly even months.
